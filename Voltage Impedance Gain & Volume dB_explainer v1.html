Audio is alternating voltage — a wave of pressure becomes a wave of electrical potential. Higher amplitude = louder sound. Different sources produce wildly different voltage levels, which is why mixers and preamps exist.

Rule of thumb: load impedance should be ≥10× the source impedance for clean voltage transfer. Mismatches cause tone coloration, signal loss, and in extreme cases, damage to the source.

Gain is the amplification factor set by the circuit (a fixed multiplier). Volume/fader attenuates the signal after amplification. Gain set too high causes clipping — the waveform hits a ceiling and distorts.

dB is a ratio, not an absolute unit. 0 dBu = 0.775 V RMS (pro standard). 0 dBV = 1 V RMS (consumer). Every +6 dB ≈ doubles the voltage; every +20 dB = 10× the voltage. The ear perceives loudness logarithmically, which is why dB maps so naturally to perceived volume.
